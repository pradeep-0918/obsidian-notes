## Q1. What is Business Forecasting?

1. **Definition** – Business forecasting is the process of **predicting future business conditions** (sales, revenue, demand, trends) using historical data, statistical methods, and analytical tools.
2. **Purpose** – Helps organizations make **informed decisions** about production, staffing, budgeting, and strategy.
3. **Types** – Qualitative (expert opinion, surveys) and Quantitative (time series, regression models).
4. **Example** – A retail store forecasting **holiday season sales** to decide how much inventory to stock in advance.

---

## Q2. Differentiate Logic-Driven and Data-Driven Models.

| Feature         | Logic-Driven Models                               | Data-Driven Models                                |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **Basis**       | Built on rules, assumptions, and domain knowledge | Built by learning patterns from historical data   |
| **Human input** | Heavy — experts define the logic                  | Minimal — algorithm learns automatically          |
| **Flexibility** | Less flexible; rigid rule structure               | Highly flexible; adapts to new data               |
| **Example**     | Decision trees with expert-defined rules          | Machine learning (Random Forest, Neural Networks) |

- **Logic-driven** = "If this, then that" — driven by human reasoning.
- **Data-driven** = "Let the data speak" — driven by statistical patterns.

## Q3. Define Predictive Analytics.

1. **Definition** – Predictive analytics is the use of **statistical algorithms, machine learning, and data mining** to forecast **future outcomes** based on historical and current data.
2. **Goal** – To answer: *"What is LIKELY to happen next?"*
3. **Techniques used** – Regression analysis, decision trees, neural networks, time series forecasting.
4. **Example** – A bank predicting which customers are **likely to default** on a loan based on past behavior and credit score patterns.

---

## Q4. What is the Role of Data Mining in Forecasting?

1. **Pattern discovery** – Data mining extracts **hidden patterns and trends** from large datasets that humans cannot detect manually.
2. **Feature identification** – Identifies which variables (features) most strongly **influence future outcomes**, improving forecast accuracy.
3. **Classification & clustering** – Groups data into segments (e.g., high-demand vs low-demand periods) to make **segment-specific forecasts**.
4. **Example** – Mining 5 years of sales data to discover that sales spike every **December and June**, helping plan seasonal inventory forecasts.

## Q5. What do you Mean by Training and Development in HR Analytics?

1. **Definition** – Training and Development in HR Analytics involves using **data analysis** to evaluate the effectiveness of employee training programs and identify skill gaps.
2. **Analytics role** – Measures metrics like **training completion rates, performance improvement scores**, and ROI of training investments.
3. **Predictive use** – Predicts which employees need upskilling based on **performance trends and role requirements**.
4. **Example** – Analyzing post-training assessment scores to determine whether a sales training program actually **improved revenue per employee**.

## Q6. State Two Analytics Applications in Supply Chain Management.

1. **Demand Forecasting** – Analytics predicts future product demand using historical sales data, market trends, and seasonal patterns to optimize **production planning and inventory levels**.
   > Example: Amazon uses demand analytics to pre-position products in warehouses near predicted high-demand areas.

2. **Logistics Optimization** – Analytics identifies the **most efficient delivery routes, carrier selection, and shipment scheduling** to reduce costs and improve delivery speed.
   > Example: FedEx uses route analytics to minimize fuel cost and delivery time.

## Q7. What is Inventory Planning?

1. **Definition** – Inventory planning is the process of **determining the optimal quantity and timing** of stock to be ordered and maintained to meet customer demand without overstocking or stockouts.
2. **Goal** – Balance between **too much stock** (high holding cost) and **too little stock** (lost sales).
3. **Analytics role** – Uses historical sales data, lead times, and demand forecasts to set **reorder points and safety stock levels**.
4. **Example** – A supermarket using analytics to ensure shelves are restocked **before** products run out, especially during peak seasons.

---

## Q8. Define Customer Behavior Analytics.

1. **Definition** – Customer Behavior Analytics is the analysis of **how customers interact with a business** — what they buy, browse, click, and how they make decisions — using data.
2. **Sources of data** – Website clickstream, purchase history, social media activity, customer service logs.
3. **Purpose** – Understand buying patterns to **personalize offers, improve retention**, and increase sales.
4. **Example** – Netflix analyzing what shows you watch, pause, and rewatch to **recommend content** tailored to your preferences.

## Q9. List Any Two Elements of the Marketing Mix.

1. **Product** – Refers to the **goods or services** offered to customers; analytics helps determine which product features drive the most satisfaction and sales.
   > Example: Analyzing reviews to find the most loved features of a smartphone.

2. **Price** – The **amount charged** for a product; pricing analytics uses competitor data, demand elasticity, and customer willingness-to-pay to set optimal prices.
   > Example: Airlines using dynamic pricing analytics to adjust ticket prices based on demand and booking time.

---

## Q10. What is the Selling Process in Marketing Analytics?

1. **Definition** – The selling process is the **step-by-step journey** a salesperson follows to convert a prospect into a customer, measured and optimized using analytics.
2. **Stages** – Prospecting → Qualifying → Presenting → Handling Objections → Closing → Follow-up.
3. **Analytics role** – Tracks **conversion rates at each stage**, identifies where prospects drop off, and predicts which leads are most likely to close.
4. **Example** – A CRM dashboard showing that 60% of leads drop off at the "Presentation" stage, signaling a need to improve product demos.

---

## Q11. What is Predictive Modeling in Business Forecasting?

1. **Definition** – Predictive modeling is the process of building a **mathematical model** using historical data to forecast future events or behaviors in a business context.
2. **Process** – Collect data → Clean data → Select model → Train model → Validate → Deploy predictions.
3. **Common models** – Linear Regression, Logistic Regression, Decision Trees, Random Forest, ARIMA (time series).
4. **Example** – A telecom company building a predictive model to forecast **how many customers will churn next month** based on usage patterns and complaints.

---

## Q12. Mention Any Two Machine Learning Algorithms Used in Forecasting.

1. **Random Forest** – An ensemble learning algorithm that builds **multiple decision trees** and combines their predictions; used for demand forecasting and customer churn prediction due to high accuracy.
   > Example: Predicting monthly product sales by combining hundreds of decision trees.

2. **LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)** – A type of **Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)** designed to learn patterns in sequential/time-series data; ideal for stock price and sales trend forecasting.
   > Example: Forecasting electricity demand over the next 30 days using past consumption patterns.

## Q13. What are the Benefits of Data-Driven Models?

1. **Objectivity** – Decisions are based on **actual data**, not gut feeling or bias, leading to more accurate and consistent outcomes.
2. **Scalability** – Data-driven models can process **millions of data points** in seconds, making them suitable for large-scale business problems.
3. **Continuous improvement** – Models **learn and improve** as more data becomes available (especially ML models).
4. **Competitive advantage** – Organizations using data-driven models respond faster to market changes and customer needs, gaining an edge over competitors.

## Q14. List Any Two Uses of Data Mining in Predictive Analytics.

1. **Customer Segmentation** – Data mining clusters customers into groups based on **similar behavior, demographics, or purchase patterns**, enabling targeted marketing predictions.
   > Example: Identifying "high-value customers likely to buy premium products."

2. **Fraud Detection** – Mining transaction data to discover **unusual patterns** that predict fraudulent activity before it causes damage.
   > Example: Credit card companies mining millions of transactions to flag anomalies predicting fraud in real time.

## Q15. Define Employee Turnover Prediction.

1. **Definition** – Employee Turnover Prediction uses **HR analytics and machine learning** to identify which employees are at **high risk of leaving** the organization in the near future.
2. **Data used** – Job satisfaction scores, performance reviews, salary data, attendance patterns, tenure, and promotion history.
3. **Goal** – Allow HR teams to **intervene proactively** (offer raises, promotions, counseling) before a valuable employee resigns.
4. **Example** – An HR analytics model flagging that employees with **no promotion in 3 years + low engagement scores** have an 80% probability of leaving within 6 months.

## Q16. What is Demand Forecasting in Supply Chain Analytics?

1. **Definition** – Demand forecasting is the process of **estimating future customer demand** for products or services using historical sales data, market trends, and analytical models.
2. **Importance** – Accurate demand forecasts prevent **overstock** (wasted capital) and **stockouts** (lost revenue and customer dissatisfaction).
3. **Methods** – Moving averages, exponential smoothing, ARIMA models, and machine learning algorithms.
4. **Example** – A clothing retailer forecasting higher demand for winter jackets in **October–December** to order stock from suppliers in advance.

## Q17. State Two Key Roles of Logistics Analytics.

1. **Route Optimization** – Logistics analytics analyzes traffic, distance, fuel cost, and delivery windows to determine the **most efficient delivery routes**, reducing cost and time.
   > Example: UPS saves millions annually using route analytics to eliminate unnecessary turns.

2. **Shipment Tracking & Performance Monitoring** – Analytics provides **real-time visibility** into shipments, tracks delivery performance (on-time rate, delays), and identifies bottlenecks in the supply chain.
   > Example: DHL using analytics dashboards to monitor fleet performance and predict delivery delays.

## Q18. What is a Conversion Funnel in Sales Analytics?

1. **Definition** – A conversion funnel is a **visual model** representing the journey customers take from initial awareness of a product to making a final purchase, with each stage narrowing down in numbers.
2. **Stages** – Awareness → Interest → Consideration → Intent → Purchase (the funnel gets narrower at each stage).
3. **Analytics role** – Measures **drop-off rates** at each stage to identify where customers are lost and what improvements are needed.
4. **Example** – An e-commerce site finding that 1000 visitors see a product, 400 add it to cart, but only 80 complete the purchase — analytics reveals a problem at the **checkout stage**.

```
1000 Visitors (Awareness)
    ↓
 600 Interested
    ↓
 400 Add to Cart
    ↓
 200 Begin Checkout
    ↓
  80 Purchase ✅ (8% conversion rate)
```

## Q19. Define Churn Prediction.

1. **Definition** – Churn Prediction is a predictive analytics technique that identifies **which customers are likely to stop using a product or service** (churn) within a given time period.
2. **Data used** – Purchase frequency, last login date, customer complaints, subscription length, and engagement scores.
3. **ML models used** – Logistic Regression, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, Neural Networks.
4. **Example** – A telecom company predicting that customers who **haven't used data for 15+ days and have raised 2+ complaints** are 75% likely to switch to a competitor.

## Q20. Mention Two KPIs Used in Marketing Analytics.

1. **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)** – Measures the **total cost to acquire one new customer** (ad spend + sales effort ÷ new customers gained); lower CAC = more efficient marketing.
   > Example: If you spent ₹50,000 and acquired 100 customers, CAC = ₹500 per customer.

2. **Return on Marketing Investment (ROMI)** – Measures the **revenue generated per rupee/dollar spent on marketing**, indicating whether campaigns are profitable.
   > Formula: ROMI = (Revenue from Campaign − Marketing Cost) ÷ Marketing Cost × 100
   > Example: Spending ₹10,000 on an ad that generates ₹40,000 revenue = 300% ROMI.
